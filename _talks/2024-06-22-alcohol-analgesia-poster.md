## Overview

Presented a research poster examining whether expectancy of alcohol analgesia moderates the association between psychological risk factors and frequency of pain self-medication using alcohol. The project focused on the CANUE model, which highlights modifiable risk factors for pain self-medication with substances, including pain-related anxiety, pain catastrophizing, negative affect, negative urgency, and expectancies about alcohol’s pain-relieving effects.

The study tested whether the relationship between CANUE-14 screening scores and self-reported frequency of pain self-medication with alcohol was strongest among individuals with elevated expectancy of alcohol analgesia. This question is relevant for alcohol and pain research because alcohol use for pain self-management is common and may contribute to negative pain-related outcomes, including chronic pain development and increased pain severity.

## Methods

This project used secondary data from **N = 428** individuals reporting regular alcohol use who were recruited through Amazon Mechanical Turk. Participants completed self-report measures assessing expectancy of alcohol analgesia, CANUE-related psychological risk factors, and frequency of pain self-medication with alcohol in the past 3 months.

A bootstrapped moderation analysis was conducted using the PROCESS macro in SPSS. The model tested whether expectancy of alcohol analgesia moderated the association between CANUE-14 screening scores and frequency of pain self-medication using alcohol. Analyses controlled for age, sex, pain severity, and alcohol risk scores from the World Health Organization Alcohol, Smoking and Substance Involvement Screening Test.

## Key Findings

The overall model was significant, **R² = .74, F(7, 420) = 170.04, p < .0001**. Expectancy of alcohol analgesia significantly moderated the relationship between CANUE-14 screening scores and self-medication behaviors, **b = 0.01, t = 4.47, SE = 0.00, p < .001**.

The relationship between CANUE-14 scores and frequency of pain self-medication using alcohol differed by level of expectancy of alcohol analgesia:

- At **low expectancy of alcohol analgesia**, CANUE-14 scores were negatively associated with self-medication frequency, **b = -0.42, t = -2.32, SE = 0.18, p = .02**.
- At **moderate expectancy of alcohol analgesia**, CANUE-14 scores were positively associated with self-medication frequency, **b = 0.31, t = 2.39, SE = 0.13, p = .02**.
- At **high expectancy of alcohol analgesia**, CANUE-14 scores showed a stronger positive association with self-medication frequency, **b = 0.62, t = 3.85, SE = 0.16, p = .0001**.

These findings supported the hypothesis that expectancy of alcohol analgesia is an important moderator of the relationship between CANUE-14 risk scores and recent pain self-medication with alcohol.

## Implications

The results suggest that assessments of risk for pain self-medication using alcohol should capture expectancy of alcohol analgesia in addition to negative pain-related attitudes, negative affect, and negative urgency. Individuals with moderate or high beliefs that alcohol relieves pain may be especially likely to show a positive relationship between psychological risk factors and self-medication frequency.

Future research should prospectively assess this relationship and evaluate whether interventions targeting expectancy of alcohol analgesia can reduce alcohol-related risk among people with pain.
