## Overview

This peer-reviewed article evaluated whether accelerometer data from consumer-grade smartwatches could be used to detect alcohol consumption behavior. The study compared a random forest classification model with a distributional algorithm to determine whether wearable sensor data could identify drinking events with enough accuracy to support future alcohol research and intervention development.

## Why It Matters

Accurately measuring alcohol use in real time is difficult. Traditional self-report methods can be affected by recall bias, participant fatigue, and missed entries, while some biological monitoring approaches may lag behind actual drinking events. Wearable sensors offer a potential way to detect drinking behavior passively and at scale, creating opportunities for more accessible monitoring tools and just-in-time interventions.

## Methods

Participants wore Android-based smartwatches while completing a structured drinking task that included water-drinking events and confounding behaviors such as touching the face, rubbing the forehead, and yawning. The study compared two approaches for detecting drinking behavior from accelerometer data:

- A random forest classification model
- A distributional algorithm

Model performance was evaluated using accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, and positive predictive value.

## Key Findings

The random forest model achieved an overall testing accuracy of **93%**, with sensitivity of **0.32**, specificity of **0.99**, and positive predictive value of **0.74**.

The distributional algorithm achieved an overall accuracy of **95%**, with sensitivity of **0.76**, specificity of **0.97**, and positive predictive value of **0.72**.

The distributional algorithm demonstrated significantly greater accuracy and sensitivity than the random forest approach, suggesting that wearable accelerometer data can effectively detect drinking behavior under controlled conditions.
